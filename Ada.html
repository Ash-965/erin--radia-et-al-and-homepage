"The world's first computer programmer"

Her Early Life Influenced By STEM

Ada was born on 10th December 1815 in London. More than a century before the launch of the world’s first electronic general purpose computers. She is commonly regarded as the world’s first programmer based on her work with Charles Babbage on his Analytical Engine.

Ada had an early start in STEM. Lady Byron, her mother, was a mathematician who was referred to as the ‘Princess of Parallelograms’ by her husband, infamous poet Lord Byron.

From age 4, Lovelace received tutoring in science and maths. Which is a remarkable course of study for a woman in the 19th Century.

Imagined A Flying Machine At Aged 12

When she was 12 her mother fell ill and went away to receive treatment. Young Ada designed a flying machine in order to travel to be with her mother. Ada loved machines and she studied the anatomy birds to figure out the mechanics of flight. She even went as far as conceptualising what powered flight might look like.

In a recorded letter to her mother she wrote, “I have got a scheme, to make a thing in the form of a horse with a steam engine in the inside so contrived as to move an immense pair of wings, fixed on the outside of the horse, in such a manner as to carry it up into the air while a person sits on its back.”

The Analytical Machine

Ada Lovelace came up with the first ever written program to make a machine function. She worked with Charles Babbage, the inventor of an early calculator that was based on a finite number of possible calculations.

Ada realised that a machine could follow a series of simple instructions, a program, to perform complex calculations.

Ada translated his work and added her own algorithm notes, which led to the development of the Analytical Engine, the earliest version of a computer. This ‘computer’ was just theoretical and was never built, so Ada never saw her programming put into practice.

HER LEGACY

She has her own day of the year. She lived a short life dying at the age of 36. However, her legacy has inspired generations of young woman and girls to study STEM and pursue careers in technology and STEM.

The second Tuesday in October is a day for recognising the achievements of women in science, technology, engineering and maths.

A programming language was named after Ada. In 1979, the U.S.Dept of Defence developed a programming language called ‘Ada’, designed to override and supersede the hundreds of different programming languages in use by the military. This language is still used today.
